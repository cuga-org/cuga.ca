Balancing Transparency & Privacy

For board members of Canadian nonprofits, effective governance requires a delicate balance. You have a right to the financial information needed to fulfill your duties, but you also have a legal and ethical obligation to protect sensitive donor information. This guide helps you navigate that crucial intersection.

What Information Can a Board Member See?

A key distinction in law exists between high-level "Financial Statements" and detailed "Accounting Records." Your access rights differ significantly for each, which is the primary mechanism for balancing oversight with privacy.

The Rules of the Road: Your Duties & Key Laws

Your actions as a board member are governed by fundamental duties and a web of federal and provincial legislation. Understanding these is essential to mitigating risk and ensuring compliance. Click each item to learn more.

1. Duty of Care: You must act with the diligence and prudence of a reasonable person in similar circumstances. This means being informed, attending meetings, reviewing financial reports, asking questions, and making decisions based on available information. Improperly restricted access to financial data can directly impede your ability to fulfill this duty.

2. Duty of Loyalty: You must act honestly, in good faith, and in the best interests of the organization, placing its interests above your own personal or financial interests. This includes avoiding and disclosing any conflicts of interest.

3. Duty of Obedience: You must ensure the organization acts in accordance with its stated mission, bylaws, and all applicable laws and regulations, including those from the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) and other governing bodies.

Corporate Acts (e.g., CNCA, ONCA): These federal and provincial laws outline the fundamental governance structure for nonprofits. They grant directors the right to inspect corporate records, mandate proper record-keeping, and set the rules for member and board meetings, director liability, and financial reporting.

Privacy Laws (PIPEDA, Provincial PIPAs, Quebec's Law 25): These laws govern how organizations collect, use, and disclose personal information. While routine fundraising may be exempt from PIPEDA, activities like selling donor lists are not. Quebec's Law 25 is stricter, applying to all organizations and all personal data processing, reinforcing the need for explicit consent and strong data security for all donor information.

Income Tax Act & CRA Rules: This act, enforced by the CRA, dictates the record-keeping requirements for registered charities, including maintaining detailed records to verify donations and expenditures. It also governs the annual T3010 information return, which makes some financial data public.

Limiting Access: How & Why It's Done

While transparency is key, restricting access to sensitive information like individual donor details is not only permissible but a critical part of good governance. This is achieved through three primary mechanisms.

Breach of Fiduciary Duty & Personal Liability

If your access to information is so restricted that you cannot fulfill your duty of care (i.e., you can't properly oversee finances), you could be held personally liable for any resulting mismanagement or non-compliance. Directors can be held accountable for problems they "knew or ought to have known" about.

Non-Compliance & Loss of Status

Failure to maintain proper financial records as required by the CRA and corporate acts can lead to severe penalties, including fines, suspension of receipting privileges, and ultimately, revocation of the organization's nonprofit or charitable status. Directors who knowingly permit such defaults can also be fined.

Privacy Breaches & Legal Action

Improperly handling or disclosing donor information, even internally among board members without a clear "need-to-know," can constitute a privacy breach. This can lead to investigations by the Privacy Commissioner, court action, class-action lawsuits, and severe reputational damage that erodes donor trust.

Disputes & Court Intervention

When financial transparency is perceived to be lacking, it can lead to internal disputes. Members or even fellow directors can apply to a court for an investigation if they believe the organization is being managed in an oppressive or improper manner, leading to costly and public legal battles.
